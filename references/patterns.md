# The Slop Pattern Catalog

Every documented AI-writing tell, with rewrites. Use this when de-slopping existing text or when the lint script flags something and you need to see what a real fix looks like. The fix is almost never a synonym swap — it's usually *add a specific* or *cut the sentence*.

## 1. Contrast scaffolds ("not X, but Y")

The most recognizable tell. AI reaches for this reflexively because it sounds profound while committing to nothing.

- ❌ "It's not just about the plants — it's about the joy they bring into your home."
- ❌ "Success isn't about working harder. It's about working smarter."
- ❌ "This isn't a setback; it's a setup for a comeback."
- ✅ Just say the second half, concretely: "People tell us the plants made their flat feel less like a rental."
- ✅ Or make the actual argument: "Working weekends didn't get me promoted. Shipping the billing rewrite did."

Variants to catch: "less about X, more about Y", "the real X isn't Y", "X isn't the goal — Y is", "not because X, but because Y" (this one humans do use — keep only if both halves carry real content).

## 2. Rule-of-three snap

Triads sound rhetorical and finished, which is why AI produces them constantly. One triad in a piece is fine. Three is a pattern.

- ❌ "Fast, simple, and reliable."
- ❌ "We planned, we built, we shipped."
- ❌ "Think bigger. Act bolder. Move faster."
- ✅ Use two, or four, or one good one: "It's fast, and it doesn't crash."
- ✅ Or replace the adjectives with the fact: "Pages load in under a second."

## 3. Unearned profundity

Dramatic narrative beats with nothing behind them. In fiction, AI does this with "Something shifted." In blogs and LinkedIn posts it's:

- ❌ "But here's the thing."  ❌ "That's when it hit me."  ❌ "Everything changed."
- ❌ "Let that sink in."  ❌ "Read that again."  ❌ "Plot twist:"
- ✅ Delete the beat and let the content be the drama: "Then the client emailed: they'd been paying for two Notion workspaces since March."

## 4. Fake question pivots

A question the writer immediately answers is a tic, not a dialogue.

- ❌ "The result? A 40% jump in orders."
- ❌ "The best part? You don't need any experience."
- ❌ "Sound familiar?"
- ✅ Fold it into a sentence: "Orders jumped 40% in the first month."
- Allowance: one genuine rhetorical question per piece, ideally one you don't answer.

## 5. Vapid openers

Throat-clearing that could open any piece on any topic.

- ❌ "In today's fast-paced digital world..."
- ❌ "Have you ever wondered..."  ❌ "Picture this:"  ❌ "We've all been there."
- ❌ "Let's face it."  ❌ "It's no secret that..."  ❌ "In an era of..."
- ✅ Open inside the material: "I cancelled my Notion subscription on a Tuesday, mid-invoice."
- ✅ Or with the claim itself: "Most bakery websites are PDFs wearing a domain name."

## 6. Universal closers and moral endings

AI cannot resist stating the lesson, then widening it to all of humanity. Humans end on the specific.

- ❌ "Whatever your 'ukulele' is — start strumming."
- ❌ "So next time you're tempted to give up, remember: growth lives outside your comfort zone."
- ❌ "Ultimately, it's not about the tools. It's about the stories we tell."
- ✅ End on a detail: "The Thai Constellation cuttings go on the shelf Saturday. I'm keeping one."
- ✅ End on a plan: "Next month I'm trying the same experiment with my invoicing."
- ✅ End on an admission: "I still open Notion sometimes. Old habits."

## 7. "No X, no Y — just Z"

- ❌ "No filters, no gimmicks — just honest coffee."
- ❌ "No teacher, no plan — just a vague sense that it would be fun."
- ✅ "We don't filter the photos because the plants look better unfiltered."

## 8. The slop lexicon

Words that spike in AI text. Any one is a yellow flag; clusters are damning. This is the *weakest* signal in the research (professionals use fancy words too), so don't contort sentences to avoid them — but never reach for them either.

**Metaphor-zone**: delve, tapestry, testament (to), landscape (metaphorical), journey (metaphorical), realm, beacon, symphony, dance (metaphorical), unlock, unleash, harness, navigate (metaphorical), embark.
**Hype-zone**: game-changer, revolutionize, seamless, effortless, elevate, empower, supercharge, next-level, cutting-edge, transformative.
**Padding-zone**: it's worth noting, it is important to note, in conclusion, in summary, moreover, furthermore, additionally (sentence-initial), that being said, needless to say, at its core, "the beauty of X is".
**Scene-setting-zone**: vibrant, bustling, nestled, boasts, whether you're a beginner or an expert, look no further.

Rewrites: the fix is always the concrete version.
- ❌ "Nestled in the heart of Delhi, our vibrant store boasts a myriad of rare plants."
- ✅ "The shop's in Shahpur Jat, down the lane past the cafe. Right now there are about 40 species on the shelves."

## 9. Emotion performed through the body

The counterintuitive one. AI "shows" emotion through physical sensation 81% of the time (humans: 38%); humans name feelings outright far more (29% vs 8%). Creative-writing advice says "show don't tell" — but in blogs, emails, and captions, telling is what humans do.

- ❌ "My heart raced as I clicked publish. A knot formed in my stomach."
- ✅ "I was genuinely scared to publish this one."
- ❌ "A wave of relief washed over me when the payment cleared."
- ✅ "The payment cleared. I was relieved." (Name it in a real sentence — a one-word "Relief." is its own tic; see section 16.)

## 10. Generic metaphors

AI metaphors are plausible-but-interchangeable; human metaphors come from specific experience or shared culture.

- ❌ "Learning ukulele is like teaching your fingers to dance."
- ❌ "Every chord is a puzzle piece that clicks into a song."
- ✅ Specific: "Barre chords feel like trying to hold a credit card flat against a rolling pin."
- ✅ Cultural: "My strumming had strong 'dad at a barbecue' energy."
- Default when unsure: no metaphor. A plain sentence never reads as slop.

## 11. Formatting slop

- Emoji-led bullet lists in professional text (✅📊💡) — never.
- Unicode arrows (→), multiplication signs (5 × day), 𝘂𝗻𝗶𝗰𝗼𝗱𝗲 styling — never.
- Random mid-paragraph **bolding** of phrases that aren't actually key — never.
- Bullets when prose would do. Bullets are for genuinely enumerable things: dates, specs, steps. If the "list items" are full sentences making an argument, write paragraphs.
- Header-per-three-sentences listicle structure — collapse into flowing sections.
- Em dashes: none at all. Not one em dash (—), spaced en dash ( – ), or double hyphen (--) in the whole piece. Replace every one with a comma, a period, parentheses, or by splitting into two sentences. The checker fails on the first occurrence, so this is zero-tolerance, not "sparingly."

## 12. Monotone rhythm

Uniform sentence length, uniform paragraph shape, unvarying cadence. The fix is mechanical and it works:

1. Find your longest paragraph. Cut its weakest sentence.
2. Find two adjacent medium sentences. Merge one pair; split another elsewhere.
3. Add one very short sentence where it lands hardest. Like this.
4. Check paragraph silhouette: if all paragraphs are 2–4 sentences, make one 1 and one 6.

## 13. Filler and over-explanation

AI says in four sentences what fits in one, and explains what the reader already understood. Both come from the same instinct: not trusting the reader.

- ❌ "Consistency is important when learning an instrument. Practicing regularly, even for short periods, helps build muscle memory. Over time, these small sessions compound into real progress. That's why daily practice matters."  (Four sentences, one idea.)
- ✅ "Five minutes a day beat my old Sunday marathons inside a month."
- The shrink test: if you can cut 25% of the words without losing content, the piece isn't done.
- Never re-state a point "in other words". Never end a paragraph by summarizing the paragraph.

## 14. Tidy resolution disease

Structural, so it survives copy-editing. AI resolves every thread, answers every question it raises, and closes symmetrical loops (intro promises three things, body delivers three things, conclusion recaps three things). Human structure is looser: a question left open, an aside never returned to, an ending that arrives a beat early. When a draft feels suspiciously complete, delete the last paragraph and see if the piece got better. It usually did.

## 15. Red herrings — do NOT "fix" these

The research is explicit that these are *not* reliable AI tells. Don't introduce errors to seem human:

- Fancy vocabulary used precisely (professionals write "ascertain" sometimes).
- Correct grammar and spelling. Never add typos deliberately.
- Formal register when the format calls for it.
- A single rhetorical question used once, deliberately, or one genuine short sentence for contrast. Good human writers own these; the tell is frequency and reflex, not existence. (Note: em dashes are the exception this skill does NOT extend that grace to — they're banned outright, because for this user zero is the rule. Rewrite, don't ration.)

## 16. Parallel and staccato repetition

The tic that outlives every other edit, because it reads as rhythm and craft while you write it. The repetition manufactures emphasis the words haven't earned. Test every instance the same way: delete the repetition; if the meaning is unchanged, it was decoration. Six forms:

**Anaphoric triads** — one word heading three parallel clauses:
- ❌ "More days mean more clicks, more conversions, and an easier path to that green badge."
- ❌ "Keep your structure tight, keep your timelines realistic, and keep your focus on the bottom line."
- ✅ "More days on the page means more chances to convert." / "Keep the structure tight and the timeline honest." (Say it once; drop the third beat that was only there for cadence.)

**Fragment stacks** — short fragments lined up for punch:
- ❌ "Fast. Simple. Done." / "No fluff. No filler. No nonsense."
- ✅ "It's fast and it doesn't crash." (One real sentence beats three fake ones.)

**Echo repetition** — repeating a word/phrase as its own beat:
- ❌ "This changes everything. Everything." / "It works. It just works."
- ✅ "This changes how the whole page loads." (If the first clause is true, the echo adds only drama.)

**Sentence-start anaphora** — consecutive sentences opening on the same word:
- ❌ "We build. We ship. We grow." / "You show up. You do the work. You leave."
- ✅ "We build the thing, ship it, and figure out growth from there." (Combine, or vary the openings.)

**One-word drama sentences** — a lone word for effect:
- ❌ "The launch was a success. Revolutionary." / "Then the room went quiet. Silence."
- ✅ "The launch worked better than we expected." (Attach it to a clause or cut it.)

**Rhetorical modifier lists** — the same comparative across items:
- ❌ "faster loads, faster checkout, faster everything."
- ✅ "The whole site loads in under a second now." (State the concrete result once.)

When editing AI-sounding text, this is often the last thing left after you've fixed the obvious tells — the piece reads clean sentence by sentence but has three "We X. We Y. We Z." runs and a couple of one-word punches. Hunt them specifically.
